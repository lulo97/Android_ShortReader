Bees are remarkable winged insects belonging to the clade Anthophila within the order Hymenoptera. With more than 20,000 known species, they represent one of the most diverse and ecologically significant groups of insects on Earth. While a few species, such as honey bees, bumblebees, and stingless bees, live in highly organized colonies, the overwhelming majority of bees lead solitary lives, independently building nests and raising their offspring. Their distinctive bodies—often marked with vivid yellow, black, or orange patterns—serve as a warning to predators while also making them among the most recognizable insects in nature.

Unlike their close relatives, wasps and ants, bees are herbivores that feed primarily on nectar and pollen from flowering plants. Nectar provides carbohydrates for energy, whereas pollen supplies the proteins and nutrients required for larval development. Bees inhabit nearly every region of the world except Antarctica, thriving wherever flowering plants grow. Their bodies are equipped with specialized structures such as branched hairs and pollen-carrying baskets that enable them to collect and transport pollen efficiently.

From an ecological perspective, bees are indispensable pollinators. As they travel from flower to flower in search of nectar and pollen, they inadvertently transfer pollen grains between plants, facilitating fertilization and the production of seeds and fruits. A substantial portion of the global food supply relies on pollination, making bees critical contributors to agriculture and biodiversity. Crops such as fruits, vegetables, and nuts depend heavily on these industrious insects for successful reproduction.

The evolutionary history of bees stretches back more than 120 million years to the Early Cretaceous period. Scientists believe that bees evolved from predatory wasps whose diets gradually shifted from insects to pollen. This transition coincided with the rapid diversification of flowering plants, leading to a profound coevolutionary relationship. Over millions of years, flowers developed features such as nectar rewards and elongated tubes, while bees evolved longer tongues and specialized pollen-collecting structures to access these resources.

Bees exhibit fascinating biological and social characteristics. Their bodies contain complex sensory systems, including compound eyes and sensitive antennae that detect smell, taste, and subtle air movements. Some species live in sophisticated eusocial societies characterized by division of labor, cooperative brood care, and distinct castes such as queens, workers, and drones. Others remain solitary, constructing nests in soil, wood, or plant stems where they provision food for their developing larvae.

Communication and navigation in bees are particularly sophisticated. Honey bees famously perform the “waggle dance,” a symbolic movement pattern that conveys the direction and distance of food sources to other members of the colony. By interpreting the angle and duration of the dance, worker bees can locate distant patches of flowers with remarkable precision. Bees also use the sun’s position, polarized light in the sky, and spatial memory to navigate complex landscapes.

Despite their ecological importance, bees face numerous threats in the modern world. Pesticides, habitat destruction, climate change, parasites, and diseases have contributed to widespread declines in both wild and domesticated bee populations. These challenges have raised serious concerns among scientists and farmers alike, as the loss of pollinators could disrupt ecosystems and compromise global food security.

Humans have interacted with bees for thousands of years, particularly through the practice of beekeeping. Ancient civilizations harvested honey from wild nests long before developing managed hives. Today, beekeepers maintain colonies not only for honey production but also for pollination services essential to modern agriculture. Beyond their economic value, bees have occupied a prominent place in mythology, literature, and symbolism, often representing diligence, cooperation, and the harmonious functioning of society.
